Fruits and vegetables are the most utilized commodities among all horticultural crops. They are consumed raw, minimally processed, as well as processed, due to their nutrients and health‐promoting compounds. With the growing population and changing diet habits, the production and processing of horticultural crops, especially fruits and vegetables, have increased very significantly to fulfill the increasing demands. Significant losses and waste in the fresh and processing industries are becoming a serious nutritional, economical, and environmental problem. For example, the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) has estimated that losses and waste in fruits and vegetables are the highest among all types of foods, and may reach up to 60%. The processing operations of fruits and vegetables produce significant wastes of by‐products, which constitute about 25% to 30% of a whole commodity group. The waste is composed mainly of seed, skin, rind, and pomace, containing good sources of potentially valuable bioactive compounds, such as carotenoids, polyphenols, dietary fibers, vitamins, enzymes, and oils, among others.

What is Composting?

Composting is a biological process during which naturally occurring microorganisms, bacteria and insects break down organic materials such as leaves, grass clippings and certain kitchen scraps into a soil-like product called compost. It is a form of recycling, a natural way of returning needed nutrients to the soil.

Why Compost?

By composting kitchen scraps and yard trimmings at home, you can conserve valuable landfill space normally used to dispose of this material and help reduce air emissions from the incinerator plants that burn garbage. In fact, if you compost on a continual basis, the volume of garbage you generate can be reduced by as much as 25%! Composting is practical, convenient and can be easier and less expensive than bagging these wastes and taking them to the landfill or transfer station.

Benefits of Using Compost.

By using compost you return organic matter and nutrients to the soil in a form readily useable to plants. Organic matter improves plant growth by helping to break heavy clay soils into a better texture, by adding water and nutrient-holding capacity to sandy soils, and by adding essential nutrients to any soil. Improving your soil is the first step toward improving the health of your plants. Healthy plants help clean our air and conserve our soil. If you have a garden, a lawn, shrubs, or even planter boxes, you have a use for compost.

WASTE SEGREGATION

Effective waste segregation is one way we can do lessen wastes reaching landfills and reduce the pollution that these wastes may bring to air, water, and land. I believe also that waste segregation always starts at home. One way we can do to segregate our wastes is to keep wet and dry wastes separately. Through this, dry wastes can be recycled and wet wastes can be composted.

In the mixture of waste materials in the garbage, the dry wastes are paper, plastic, glass, scrap iron, and aluminum. These wastes can be changed recycled by collecting and changing materials into new products instead of throwing them away. There are many ways we can do to recycle these materials. On the other hand, wet wastes such as animal and vegetable wastes can be composted. Compost will help in the improvement of soil thus, providing plant nutrients such as nitrogen, and phosphorus. Also, making compost keeps these materials out of landfills that may contribute to greenhouse gas.

These are simple steps that we can do to segregate wastes at home. We just don't help in reducing wastes but we also help in reducing harm to our environment. Thus, it is important that each one of us do our part in reducing wastes by doing segregation.
