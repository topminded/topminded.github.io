3. It is an expanded paper that presents interpretations and analysis of a phenomenon based on experiments and previous information


Research Report

An expanded paper called a research report provides readers with interpretations and analyses of a phenomenon based on experiments and prior knowledge so they can better understand it. The goal of a research report is to give interested parties a thorough explanation of all findings from the investigation, allowing them to assess the reliability of the findings for themselves. The research report, which serves as the summary of the investigation, includes a description of the various phases of the survey and the conclusions drawn. As a result, it is a byproduct of research that provides a narrative of a protracted search for new or modified knowledge.

A good research report not only spreads knowledge but also presents the findings to broaden people's perspectives on it. Aside from that, it also verifies the accuracy of the generalization and motivates others to work on related or allied issues. Under the following headings, the research report's goal may be discussed:

1. Information exchange

For proper use of the resources invested, the knowledge that has been acquired through study needs to be transmitted. For this reason, it is always advisable to prepare reports in writing so that the general public can benefit from the information by better understanding a variety of social issues.

2. Presentation of the results

In terms of research output, the finished product is more important to society because it represents a significant investment of time, money, and human resources. The study report's social value therefore lies in both its dissemination to the general public and its submission to the project's funding source.

3. Inspiration for further research

Others are motivated by study reports to conduct additional studies along the same lines or in related multidisciplinary areas. The likelihood that social scientists will pay attention to a report increases if it seems innovative and engaging.

Creating multimodal texts

Multimodal texts combine two or more modes such as written language, spoken language, visual (still and moving image), audio, gestural, and spatial meaning (The New London Group, 2000; Cope and Kalantzis, 2009). Creating digital multimodal texts involves use of communication technologies, however, multimodal texts can also be paper based, or live performances.

The Victorian Curriculum recognises that students need to be able to create a range of increasingly complex and sophisticated spoken, written, and multimodal texts for different purposes and audiences, with accuracy, fluency and purpose.
