2. three or more solution/programs that address global poverty​


Answer:

1. EQUALITY AND REPRESENTATION FOR ALL

One of the main causes of extreme poverty is marginalization — the systemic barriers that lead to groups of people going without representation in their communities. In order for a community or country to work its way out of poverty, all groups must be involved in the decision-making process — especially when it comes to having a say in the things that determine your place in society.

2. BUILDING RESILIENCE — CLIMATE AND OTHERWISE

Poverty is most likely to occur when there is a high combination of marginalization and risk — with risk being its own combination of a person or group’s level of vulnerability and the hazards they face. For instance, the DRC has suffered ongoing conflict since declaring independence from France in 1960.

3. BUT ESPECIALLY FOCUSING ON CLIMATE CHANGE

Resilience against climate change is especially important and deserves its own mention. According to the World Bank, climate change could force an additional 100 million people into extreme poverty over the next decade without any urgent action taken.

4. INCREASE ACCESS TO EDUCATION

According to UNESCO, if all students in low-income countries had just basic reading skills (nothing else), an estimated 171 million people could escape extreme poverty. If all adults completed secondary education, we could cut the global poverty rate by more than half. Education develops skills and abilities, corrects some of the imbalances that come out of marginalization, and decreases both risk and vulnerability.

5. IMPROVE FOOD SECURITY AND ACCESS TO CLEAN WATER

Simply eating three meals a day and getting a healthy amount of calories and nutrients can go a long way to addressing the cycle of poverty. When a person doesn’t have enough to eat, they lack the strength and energy needed to work. Contaminated water can lead to debilitating illnesses.

6. END WAR AND CONFLICT

No war means that budgets allocated to cover the cost of conflict can be used to deliver public services. It also reduces risks faced by the most vulnerable communities, and ensures that goals towards equality and inclusion can be maintained.

7. EMBRACE CASH AND MICROFINANCE

One of the ways Cambodia’s transition from wartime to peace (including the repatriation of over 300,000 Cambodian refugees) was so smooth was thanks to the idea of buying on credit. While such an influx of returnees could place a strain on resources and create financial dire straits, micro financing models introduced into the country helped to establish savings, loans, insurance, and cash transfer services in communities that need them the most, allowing people to purchase the tools and services they need in order to become self-sufficient. Between 1998 and 2018, Cambodia’s economy grew by an average of 8% each year, and its middle class began to flourish.

15. ways to address global inequality​


Answer:

Today, more than ever, the key to reversing this inequality trend lies clearly in improving the employment situation. Unemployment during the crisis has reached unprecedented levels. We currently have close to 49 million people unemployed in the OECD area, nearly 14 million more than before the crisis. The young have been particularly affected with 16.5% of them out of work in the OECD. This rate even surpasses 50% in countries such as Greece and Spain. Look at Italy: your unemployment rate is 11.5%, while youth unemployment currently stands at 38.4%!

We need to urgently focus on creating more and better jobs and on offering real career prospects for these youth. We must, for example, focus on up-skilling the workforce and removing barriers to higher labour force participation of women; thereby strengthening gender equality.

The provision of public services such as education and health is also essential in reducing inequality. Improving the quality of these services is therefore crucial. With PISA, the OECD provides a powerful tool to assess the quality of education and the experience of 34 OECD member states and 37 partner countries to improve on it.

The reform of tax and benefit policies is also central to our goals. Over the last two decades, many countries moved away from highly progressive income tax rates and net wealth taxes. Viable and effective policy avenues do exist, such as: raising marginal tax rates on the highest income brackets; improving tax compliance; eliminating tax exemptions; and reassessing the role of property and wealth taxes.

Inequalities are threatening the viability of our economies and eroding social trust. People are increasingly calling into question the strength of the “social compact”. Re-igniting growth and putting people back to work is our utmost priority, but this has to be implemented and achieved in a sustainable and inclusive manner. To achieve this, we must ensure that the young receive the job security they deserve; that our fiscal policies ensure that everyone is paying their fair share; that gender equality in the workplace is never questioned; and that the benefits of economic growth do trickle down!


16. List down five (5) examples of global/international inequalities and suggest ways on how the government should address each of them. GLOBAL/INTERNATIONAL INEQUALITIES POSSIBLE SOLUTION​


Answer:

Income inequality: Many countries have a significant gap between the rich and the poor, with a small percentage of the population holding a disproportionate amount of wealth. The government can address this by implementing progressive taxation policies and increasing social welfare programs to redistribute wealth and provide basic needs to all citizens.Education inequality: Some countries have inadequate education systems, resulting in a lack of access to quality education for certain groups of people. The government can address this by increasing funding for education and providing scholarships or financial assistance for underprivileged students.Gender inequality: Many countries still have significant gender disparities, especially in terms of equal pay and representation in leadership positions. The government can address this by implementing policies that promote gender equality, such as affirmative action and quotas for women in leadership positions.Health inequality: Some countries have inadequate healthcare systems, resulting in poor access to healthcare for certain groups of people. The government can address this by investing in public health programs and providing universal healthcare coverage to all citizens.Environmental inequality: Some countries have poor environmental conditions, resulting in poor air and water quality and a lack of access to clean energy. The government can address this by implementing policies that promote sustainable development and clean energy, such as carbon taxes and subsidies for renewable energy.

It's worth noting that addressing these inequalities will require a multifaceted approach, including collaboration among governments, private sector, and civil society organizations.

Local: Encouraging community involvement and empowering marginalized groups to participate in local decision-making processes.

National: Promoting equal representation and inclusivity in government policies and programs.

Global: Advocating for global agreements and policies that promote equality and address systemic inequalities.

Ethnic Minority:

Local: Addressing discrimination through education and community outreach programs, promoting cultural diversity and inclusion.

National: Implementing anti-discrimination laws and policies, promoting equal access to resources and opportunities.

Global: Collaborating with international organizations to address systemic inequalities and promote cultural understanding and respect.

Gender:

Local: Promoting gender equality through education and awareness-raising programs, addressing gender-based violence and discrimination.

National: Implementing laws and policies to promote gender equality in all sectors, including the workplace and political representation.

Global: Advocating for global agreements and policies that promote gender equality and women's rights.

Local: Addressing discrimination and promoting inclusion for marginalized groups, such as the LGBTQ+ community and people with disabilities.

Global Inequalities:

Global: Collaborating with international organizations to address systemic inequalities and promote sustainable and equitable economic development.

International Inequalities:

Local: Supporting international development programs that address poverty, education, and healthcare needs.

National: Contributing to international aid and development efforts, promoting fair trade and economic policies.
