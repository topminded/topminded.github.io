7. Which graph shows the equation V = 4 + 2t, where V is the total volume of water in a bucket and t is the elapsed time in minutes?


Hi Peaceyow1535,

This equation that you have given is a linear equation in its slope intercept form. Typically, this type of function always form a straight line that passes to what it is called the y-intercept. Let us understand first the nature of this equation shall we?

The slope-intercept form

This form of linear equation reveals itself in this format.

[tex]y=mx+b[/tex]

such that

y = dependent variable

x = independent variable

m = slope

b = y-intercept

The y-intercept

In essence the y-intercept is the resulting value of y if the value of x is zero (0). Evidently, as the graph of the line equation approaches the y-axis it will cross to the said y-intercept.

The slope

Slope is the steepness of a line. It is defined mathematically as rise over run. This can be simplified through this equation.

[tex]slope=\frac{rise}{run}[/tex]

It needs a fraction to easily define a slope. For example if the slope is [tex]\frac{1}{2}[/tex] it means that for every 2 units along the x-axis, there is a resulting 1 unit to be plotted along the y-axis.

However, if the slope is a whole number, we can rephrase the whole number form to fraction form by placing 1 as the denominator. This holds true because any number divided by 1 is the same number. For example, if the slope is 3 we can rephrase it by [tex]\frac{3}{1}[/tex]. This means that for every 1 unit that run along x-axis there is a resulting rise of 3 units along the y-axis.

How do these work?

Now that you have understood the two essential factors to describe the graph of the line, you can plot it without manually computing the values of y for every values of x. Such that for your problem

[tex]V=4+2t[/tex]

can be rephrased to follow the slope-intercept form

[tex]V=2t+4[/tex]

From that point you may now know that

the slope of the line is [tex]\frac{2}{1}[/tex] ;andthe y-intercept is 4

15. Mr. Meadows has a 6 1⁄2 gallon bucket. His dipping can holds 1 1⁄2 gallons. How many times does he have to dip into the pond with the dipping can to fill the bucket to the top?


To fill a 6 1/2 gallon bucket with a 1 1/2 gallon dipping can, we need to find how many times the dipping can needs to be filled and emptied into the bucket.

First, we need to convert 6 1/2 gallons to a mixed number with a common denominator of 2:

6 1/2 = (6 x 2/2) + 1/2 = 12/2 + 1/2 = 13/2

Next, we need to divide the total volume of the bucket by the volume of the dipping can:

13/2 ÷ 3/2 = 13/2 x 2/3 = 26/6 = 4 remainder 2/6

The answer shows that Mr. Meadows needs to fill and empty the dipping can into the bucket four times, with a remainder of 2/6 (or 1/3) of a can left to fill. He can either dip the can a fifth time to fill up the remaining 1/3 or find a smaller container to scoop up the remaining water.
