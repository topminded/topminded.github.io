8. Projects for environmental issues that is done by the Philippines government​


Explanation:

Projects and Commitments

Chemical Information Exchange Network

CIEN is a project launched by UNEP in 2000. The project helped to establish national and regional networks,and provided training and equipment, which aims to: eliminate barriers to information exchange; Facilitate access to technical information on chemicals on the internet; create synergies between national information of chemical management;. strengthen national capacity for the environmentally sound management of chemicals and participation in international activities and agreement.

Non-Com POPs Project

This Philippine Project aims to use non-combustion technology to destroy significant obsolete PCBs wastes and help remove barriers to the further adoption and effective implementation of the selected technology and meet the Stockholm Convention requirements is part of a Global Programme.The immediate project objective is to deploy a commercially available, proven non- combustion technology to address 1,500 tonnes of PCBs-containing equipment and wastes. The currently known PCB inventories reported in the NIP of the Stockholm Convention dated January 2006 include 6,879 tonnes of PCB containing equipment and wastes comprising about 2,400 tonnes of PCBs oil.

Capacity Development Project on Water Quality Management

Project Purpose:

Capabilities of EMB Central and Regional Offices to implement priority actions mandated under the CWA IRR are strengthened.

Overall goal:

Under initiatives of the WQMA Governing Board, industries, commercial entities, LGUs, and other public organizations take necessary actions for achieving the water quality goal established in the WQMA Action Plans.

Manila Bay Environmental Management Project

The Manila Bay Environmental Management Project (MBEMP) is a local component of the Regional Programme on Building Partnerships in Environmental Management for the Seas of East Asia (PEMSEA), a UNDP-GEF funded project with the Department of Environment and Natural Resources (DENR) as the host institution in the Philippines and the International Maritime Organization (IMO) as the executing agency. It will develop and implement a Operational Plan for Manila Bay Coastal Strategy(OPMBCS) in partnership with national government agencies and stakeholders both from public and private sectors.

ADB-Environmental Impact Assessment Technical Assistance

The TA supports the country’s response to the global call for ownership, harmonization, alignment, results and mutual accountability under the Paris Declaration of March 2005.

Primary Objective of the TA:

To enhance effectiveness and efficiency of development by:

1. Encouraging rationalized and simplified procedures for better implementation of development projects;

2. Aligning development partner activities with the Government’s planning cycle and development agenda; and 3.Harmonizing the development Partners’ policies and procedures among themselves and with those of the government to minimize costs and delay.


9. What are the issues in the horticulture industry in the Philippines? What government programs/interventions were implemented to address these issues? ​


Answer:

The issues in the horticulture industry in the Philippines include inadequate agricultural support, low access to credit, weak supply chain, limited access to modern farming technology, and pests and diseases. To address these issues, the government implemented various interventions, such as training farmers on good agricultural practices, providing seed and planting material grants, launching crop insurance programs, and building the infrastructure for crop marketing and market linkages.

Answer:

The Philippine response to COVID-19 has been described as being one of the longest and strictest lockdowns in the world. Why has the Philippine government relied heavily on draconian measures in its “war” against COVID-19? And what discourse informed the framing of its response as a war against the virus? This article argues that the government’s reliance on draconian measures was a consequence of securitising COVID-19, appreciating the virus as an “existential threat.” The securitisation of COVID-19 was reinforced with a narrative characterising the situation of the country as being at war against an “unseen enemy.” This war-like narrative, however, invariably produced a subject, the pasaway. As the perpetual enemy of health and order, the pasaway became the target of disciplining and policing. The targeting of the pasaway was informed by deep-seated class prejudices and Duterte’s authoritarian tendencies

create a synthesis of the different mandates the Philippines government initiated to address to global climate issues​

The Philippines is dedicated to addressing the issue of climate change. United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, both of which set objectives for reducing global greenhouse gas emissions.

These included encouraging energy efficiency, upgrading transportation systems, switching to cleaner fuels and vehicles, improving solid waste management and wastewater treatment, assuring forest conservation, restoration, and rehabilitation, and implementing climate-resilient agriculture techniques.

The National Framework Strategy on Climate Change (NSFCC) sets the groundwork for national and local policies aimed at creating a climate-resilient Philippines with healthy, safe, wealthy, and self-sufficient communities as well as vibrant and productive ecosystems.

The NFSCCC is a risk-based organization, which means that plans and choices must be based on the sources, amount, and effect of risks. It also encourages proactive adaptation strategies to mitigate the effects of climate change.

The anchor approach is adaptation, and mitigation efforts will be undertaken as a result of adaptation. The Philippine Agenda 21 for Sustainable Development is adopted.
