6. how does seismograph work and what does it works​


Answer:

Seismographs record seismic waves. They contain a weight and a pen attached to a spring (below). The seismograph is bolted to the ground so during an earthquake it moves with the ground, whilst the weight and pen remain still. The pen moves across a rotating paper roll recording the seismic waves.

13. How does Earth's interior work and how does it affect the surface?


The Earth's interior varies in density and it also varies in the basic kinds of matter.

The convection current which is the movement of the mantle's fluid layer can cause the tectonic plates to move.

As the magma heats from the inner core, it rises up and as it reaches the upper part of the mantle, it cools down, causing it to go back down. This creates many convection currents.

When the cooling lava meets in the upper mantle, it pushes towards each other. The tectonic plates above them therefore converge and create mountains and mountain ranges.

The opposite of this is a divergent. It creates deep ocean trenches and ravines.

Next, the inner core is also known to affect the Earth's magnetic Northern and Southern poles.

Lastly, the most common way that the interior of the Earth affects the surface is through a volcanic eruption.

Answer:

1.The musculoskeletal system is made up of the body's bones (the skeleton), muscles, cartilage, tendons, ligaments, joints, and other connective tissue that supports and binds tissues and organs together. Its primary functions include supporting the body, allowing motion, and protecting vital organs.

2.Muscles work by contracting and relaxing. Muscles have long, thin cells that are grouped into bundles. When a muscle fiber gets a signal from its nerve, proteins and chemicals release energy to either contract the muscle or relax it. When the muscle contracts, this pulls the bones it's connected to closer together.

3.Although they're very light, bones are strong enough to support our entire weight. Bones also protect the organs in our bodies. The skull protects the brain and forms the shape of the face. The spinal cord, a pathway for messages between the brain and the body, is protected by the backbone, or spinal column.

4.Tendon, tissue that attaches a muscle to other body parts, usually bones. Tendons are the connective tissues that transmit the mechanical force of muscle contraction to the bones; the tendon is firmly connected to muscle fibres at one end and to components of the bone at its other end.

5.Ligaments often connect two bones together, particularly in the joints: Like strong, firmly attached straps or ropes, they stabilize the joint or hold the ends of two bones together. This ensures that the bones in the joint don't twist too much or move too far apart and become dislocated.

How does electricity work step by step?

The rapidly rotating magnets are pushing the electrons around, creating a magnetic field and a flow of electrons that move down the copper wire. The current moves to the power grid transmission lines and electrical substations then to our homes and businesses. This flow of electrons is what makes up the electricity.
