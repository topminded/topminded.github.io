12. Determine the color of the organism at the different stages of Gram staining.1. Crystal VioletIn gram positive:In gram negative:2. IodineIn gram positive:In gram negative:3. AlcoholIn gram positive:In gram negative:4. Safranin OIn gram positive:In gram negative:​


Answer:

1.Crystal Voilet is a type of stain used in microbiology to distinguish between gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria.

When used in the Gram staining procedure, crystal violet stains the cell walls of both gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria, but the subsequent decolorization step differentiates the two types of bacteria.

In gram-positive bacteria, the thick peptidoglycan layer of the cell wall retains the crystal violet stain, resulting in a purple color.

In contrast, gram-negative bacteria have a thinner peptidoglycan layer and an outer membrane that contains lipopolysaccharides. During the decolorization step, the alcohol-based solution removes the crystal violet stain from the thinner peptidoglycan layer of gram-negative bacteria, leaving them colorless. A counterstain, such as safranin, is then used to stain the decolorized gram-negative bacteria pink or red.

2. Iodine is a type of stain used in microbiology to distinguish between gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria.

When used in the Gram staining procedure, iodine is applied after crystal violet and forms complexes with the crystal violet, making it insoluble in water. This step serves to lock in the crystal violet stain, preventing it from being washed out during subsequent washing steps.

In gram-positive bacteria, the thick peptidoglycan layer of the cell wall retains the crystal violet-iodine complex, resulting in a purple color.

In contrast, gram-negative bacteria have a thinner peptidoglycan layer and an outer membrane that contains lipopolysaccharides. During the decolorization step, the alcohol-based solution removes the crystal violet-iodine complex from the thinner peptidoglycan layer of gram-negative bacteria, leaving them colorless. A counterstain, such as safranin, is then used to stain the decolorized gram-negative bacteria pink or red.

3.Alcohol, specifically ethanol or isopropanol, is used in microbiology as a decolorizing agent in the Gram staining procedure to distinguish between gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria.

In gram-positive bacteria, the thick peptidoglycan layer of the cell wall retains the crystal violet-iodine complex after staining, resulting in a purple color. When alcohol is applied, it dehydrates the peptidoglycan layer and tightens it, making it more difficult for the crystal violet-iodine complex to be removed. As a result, gram-positive bacteria typically retain the purple color.

In gram-negative bacteria, the thinner peptidoglycan layer and outer membrane containing lipopolysaccharides allows the crystal violet-iodine complex to be easily washed out during the decolorization step with alcohol. This results in the gram-negative bacteria losing the purple color. A counterstain, such as safranin, is then used to stain the decolorized gram-negative bacteria pink or red.

4. Safranin O is a type of counterstain used in the Gram staining procedure to visualize gram-negative bacteria.

In gram-positive bacteria, the thick peptidoglycan layer of the cell wall retains the crystal violet-iodine complex after staining, resulting in a purple color. Therefore, safranin is not necessary for staining gram-positive bacteria, as they already appear purple.

In contrast, gram-negative bacteria have a thinner peptidoglycan layer and an outer membrane containing lipopolysaccharides. During the decolorization step with alcohol, the crystal violet-iodine complex is removed from the thinner peptidoglycan layer, leaving gram-negative bacteria colorless. Safranin is then used to stain the decolorized gram-negative bacteria pink or red, allowing them to be visualized
