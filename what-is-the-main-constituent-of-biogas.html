Although natural antioxidants (notably lecithin and tocopherols) give limited protection to the fat in the bean, oxidation may still occur. Oxidation is similar to that of lipase on the triglycerides but is a little more complex. Instead of splitting off the whole fatty acid, oxygen will attack that part of the fatty acids containing a double bond, producing oxidative rancidity in the fat. Not all fatty acids contain double-bonded carbon atoms, but those that do, like oleic acid (see Table 9.1), are called unsaturated. Oxygen will more readily attack the unsaturated acids in the presence of light, some metals, moisture and heat. Copper is one such metal and needs to be avoided in factory-piping, including the valves. The last two factors, moisture and heat, are very important for the storage of cocoa. Not only do low levels of each inhibit the growth of microbiological activity but they also reduce the level of oxidation. Naturally the fewer double bonds or unsaturated fatty acids present in the butter, the lower the chance of oxidation taking place. Biogas is gasoline or fuel products derived from biological matter and not fossil fuels. Biogas is usually ideally from waste biological products, as it is ideal to give value to waste products which do not have value.
