Step-by-step explanation:

1. The effective interest method is used to discount, or write off, a bond.

2. The amount of the bond discount is amortized to interest expense over the bond's life. As a bond's book value increases, the amount of interest expense increases.

3. The effective interest method considers the impact of the bond purchase price rather than accounting only for its par value or face value.

4. For lenders or investors, the effective interest rate reflects the actual return far better than the nominal rate.

5. For borrowers, the effective interest rate shows costs more effectively.

6. Unlike the real interest rate, the effective interest rate does not account for inflation.

15. The premium on an insurance policy is 20% for the first year, 15% on the second year, and 10% for the third year. Find the total premium paid for a policy of $40,000.


Answer:

The first year premium would be 20% of $40,000, or $8,000.

The second year premium would be 15% of $40,000, or $6,000.

The third year premium would be 10% of $40,000, or $4,000.

The total premium paid over the three years would be $8,000 + $6,000 + $4,000 = $18,000.

Step-by-step explanation:

1. First, calculate the premium for the first year by multiplying the policy amount by the percentage: $40,000 x 20% = $8,000

2. Next, calculate the premium for the second year by multiplying the policy amount by the percentage: $40,000 x 15% = $6,000

3. Then, calculate the premium for the third year by multiplying the policy amount by the percentage: $40,000 x 10% = $4,000

4. Finally, add up the premiums from each year to find the total premium paid: $8,000 + $6,000 + $4,000 = $18,000
