24. how are you protected In case of blood donation and blood transfusion​


Answer:

Blood donors are asked a set of standard questions prior to donating blood to assist in determining if they are in good health and free of any diseases that could be transmitted by blood transfusion. If the donor’s answers indicate they are not well or are at risk for having a disease transmissible by blood transfusion, they are not allowed to donate blood.

If the donor is eligible to donate, the donated blood is tested for blood type (ABO group) and Rh type (positive or negative). This is to make sure that patients receive blood that matches their blood type. Before transfusion, the donor and blood unit are also tested for certain proteins (antibodies) that may cause adverse reactions in a person receiving a blood transfusion.

All blood for transfusion is tested for evidence of certain infectious disease pathogens, such as hepatitis B and C viruses and human immunodeficiency virus (HIV).

30. Deinfe Blood Donation?


Blood donation refers to a voluntary act of a person drawing blood from him/her for current or for future medical use such as blood transfusion, fractionation (breaking down of a whole blood component into fractions) blood samples for testing and many other related purposes.  Donation may be of whole blood or of specific four (4) blood components such as red blood cell, white blood cell, platelets and plasma.

 

Donation may be done paid or unpaid. In third world countries, limited supply of blood forces other family relatives to donate blood for their sick family members in exchange to the blood available in the blood bank.  Many blood donors donate as an act of charity, and in some cases there are incentives for donating such as paid time off from work.
