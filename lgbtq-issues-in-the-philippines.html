5. Do you think equal employee rights are given to men, women, and members of the LGBTQ community in the Philippines? Justify


Answer:

While there have been significant efforts to promote equality and non-discrimination in the workplace in the Philippines, there is still a long way to go in terms of ensuring equal employee rights for men, women, and members of the LGBTQ community.

The Philippine Constitution guarantees equal protection under the law regardless of sex, gender identity, or sexual orientation. The Labor Code of the Philippines also prohibits employment discrimination on the basis of sex, age, race, religion, and other factors. Additionally, there are several laws that specifically address gender equality, such as the Anti-Sexual Harassment Act, the Magna Carta of Women, and the Expanded Maternity Leave Law.

However, despite these legal protections, discrimination and harassment continue to be problems in the workplace. Women, in particular, still face significant challenges in terms of equal pay, access to promotion and leadership positions, and protection from sexual harassment. Members of the LGBTQ community also face discrimination and harassment, with no specific laws protecting them from discrimination in the workplace.

Overall, while there have been some strides towards ensuring equal employee rights in the Philippines, there is still much work to be done to fully realize the principles of equality and non-discrimination in the workplace.

9. Topic sentence of "LGBTQ in the Philippines"


Answer:

Schools should be safe places for everyone. But in the Philippines, students who are lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgender (LGBT) too often find that their schooling experience is marred by bullying, discrimination, lack of access to LGBT-related information, and in some cases, physical or sexual assault. These abuses can cause deep and lasting harm and curtail students’ right to education, protected under Philippine and international law.

In recent years, lawmakers and school administrators in the Philippines have recognized that bullying of LGBT youth is a serious problem, and designed interventions to address it. In 2012, the Department of Education (DepEd), which oversees primary and secondary schools, enacted a Child Protection Policy designed to address bullying and discrimination in schools, including on the basis of sexual orientation and gender identity. The following year, Congress passed the Anti-Bullying Law of 2013, with implementing rules and regulations that enumerate sexual orientation and gender identity as prohibited grounds for bullying and harassment. The adoption of these policies sends a strong signal that bullying and discrimination are unacceptable and should not be tolerated in educational institutions.
