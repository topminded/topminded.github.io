How are rainbows formed?

A rainbow is caused by sunlight and atmospheric conditions. Light enters a water droplet, slowing down and bending as it goes from air to denser water. The light reflects off the inside of the droplet, separating into its component wavelengths or colors. When light exits the droplet, it makes a rainbow.

What is a rainbow?

A rainbow is a multicolored arc formed by the collision of light with water droplets. The most common sort of rainbow occurs when sunlight strikes raindrops at a specific angle in front of a spectator (42 degrees). Rainbows can be seen in the presence of fog, sea spray, or waterfalls.

Types of rainbow

Fog bow

-A fogbow is a sort of rainbow that appears when sunlight passes through fog or a tiny cloud. The fog's moisture droplets work together to deflect the light. This form of the rainbow is most common in regions where the air is clear. It can form above or below any body of water.

Lunar

-This type of rainbow can only emerge when the moon is nearly fully illuminated. It appears as a white arc when it does so. The moon's rim is dotted with lunar rainbows. They have a drab appearance because to the fact that the moon's light is not as brilliant as the earth's.

Multiple Rainbows

- Multiple or double rainbows are one of the rarest types. They happen when numerous rainbows appear at the same time in the same spot. This location is made up of at least one primary rainbow as well as multiple secondary rainbows. Each one is always separated by a gap.

Twinned

- It's equally uncommon to observe a twinned rainbow. Despite the fact that they share a common basis, two rainbows emerge, one primary and the other secondary. Both rainbows have the same colors in the same order.

Full Circle

- This sort of rainbow is most common at high elevations. The sun's position at lower altitudes prohibits the formation of a complete circle.

Supernumerary bow

- Another uncommon kind is supernumerary bows. It can be found in primary rainbows, where it acts as a second band within the main band. This type is formed when sunlight strikes water drops that are 1 millimeter in diameter or less.

Monochrome

- A red rainbow, also known as a monochromatic rainbow, can be seen most often during sunrise or dusk. Sunlight travels deeper in the atmosphere during this period, scattering shorter wavelengths (blue and violet). In this rainbow, only the long-wavelength red colors are visible.
