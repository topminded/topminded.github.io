2. why is it important to maintain social equilibrium?​


Answer:

Social equilibrium, a theoretical state of balance in a social system referring both to an internal balance between interrelated social phenomena and to the external relationship the system maintains with its environment. It is the tendency of the social system, when disturbed, to return to its original state, because any small change in a social element is followed by changes in other related elements that work toward diminishing the first disturbance.

9. suggest management strategies to maintain the watersheds state of equilibrium.​


Answer:

Here are a few management strategies that can be used to maintain the state of equilibrium in a watershed:

Watershed protection and management: Implementing measures to protect the watershed from human activities that can lead to degradation, such as logging, mining, and urbanization.Soil conservation: Implementing measures to reduce soil erosion and sedimentation, such as terracing, contour farming, and the construction of check dams.Water conservation: Implementing measures to conserve water and reduce the risk of floodings, such as rainwater harvesting, and irrigation management.Reforestation and afforestation: Planting trees to increase vegetation cover and reduce soil erosion, and help in the maintenance of water quality.Community engagement and education: Involving the local community in the management of the watershed, and educating them about the importance of preserving and protecting the watershed.Monitoring and assessment: Regularly monitoring and assessing the health of the watershed, including water quality, vegetation cover, and soil erosion, to identify and address any issues that arise.Watershed restoration: Restoring degraded areas of the watershed, through activities such as soil stabilization, stream and wetland restoration, and the removal of invasive plant species.Integrated Watershed Management: Using an integrated and holistic approach to manage the entire watershed including, physical, chemical, and biological aspects of the watershed.

25. it is the ability or tendency of an organism to maintain internal equilibrium by regulating its processes​


Characteristics of Homeostatic Systems

Homeostasis refers to the ability of an organism to maintain the internal environment of the body within limits that allow it to survive. Homeostasis also refers to self-regulating processes that return critical systems of the body to a set point within a narrow range of operation, consistent with survival of the organism. Homeostasis is highly developed in warm-blooded animals living on land, which must maintain body temperature, fluid balance, blood pH, and oxygen tension within rather narrow limits, while at the same time obtaining nutrition to provide the energy to maintain homeostasis. This is because maintaining homeostasis requires the expenditure of energy. Energy is used for locomotion, as the animal seeks and consumes food and water, for maintaining body temperature via the controlled release of calories from metabolism of food or fat stores, and for sustaining cell membrane function as it resorbs electrolytes in the kidney and intestine and maintains neutral blood pH. Homeostasis also refers to the body's defensive mechanisms. These include protective reflexes against such things as inhaling matter into the lungs, the vomiting reflex as a protection to expel toxic materials from the esophagus or stomach, the eye blink reflex, and the withdrawal response to hot or otherwise painful skin sensations. There is also the defense against pathogens through innate and acquired immunity, the latter of which is stimulated by acute stress via cortisol and adrenalin and inhibited by chronic stress and high levels of cortisol.3,4

Answer:

The term, homeostasis, refers to an organism's ability to regulate various physiological processes to keep internal states steady and balanced.
