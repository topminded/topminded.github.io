The noun draught is pronounced exactly like draft, and it also shares most of the same meanings. A cold burst of wind, a swig or a serving of a drink, the act of pulling a heavy load, and the depth of a ship below the surface of the water: each of these can be called a draught.


4. Explain the thematic concerns explored by Caribbean women writers in Da-duh in Memoriam and The Draught by Sylvia Wynter.​


PA BRAINLIEST

Answer:

"Da-duh in Memoriam" by Paule Marshall and "The Draught" by Sylvia Wynter are two short stories written by Caribbean women writers that explore the experiences and struggles of Caribbean people, particularly women, in the colonial and postcolonial eras.

In "Da-duh in Memoriam," the story is narrated by a young girl who visits her grandmother in Barbados for the first time. The story explores the themes of identity, colonialism, and cultural conflict. The grandmother, Da-duh, is proud of her country and its independence from British colonialism, while the young girl is more interested in the modern American culture she is used to. Through their conversations, the story highlights the cultural divide between the old and the new, the traditional and the modern, and the struggles of coming to terms with one's identity.

In "The Draught," the story centers around a poor Jamaican family struggling to survive during a severe drought. The story explores themes of poverty, social injustice, and the struggles of everyday life in a society that is not always just. The story highlights the plight of women who are often forced to bear the brunt of poverty and social injustice, as well as the resilience of people who are able to find hope and strength in the face of adversity.

In both stories, the writers highlight the experiences of Caribbean women in a society that is often patriarchal and oppressive. They explore themes of identity, cultural conflict, poverty, and social injustice, and the struggles of Caribbean people to survive and find meaning in a changing world. These themes are still relevant today, as Caribbean people continue to navigate the challenges of postcolonialism and globalization.

24. Explain the major thematic concerns and techniques explored by Sylvia Wynter in 'The Draught.'​


Explanation:

Sylvia Wynter's essay "The Draught" explores the themes of power, domination, and resistance in the context of colonialism and post-colonialism. The essay critiques the hegemonic narratives that sustain the colonial enterprise and proposes an alternative perspective that foregrounds the agency and subjectivity of the colonized.

The important theme in "The Draught" is the idea of resistance, which Wynter sees as a crucial aspect of the struggle against colonialism. She argues that resistance can take many forms, including armed struggle, political mobilization, cultural revitalization, and intellectual critique. However, she also recognizes the limits and challenges of resistance, particularly in the face of the overwhelming power of colonialism and its global reach.

In terms of techniques, Wynter employs a wide range of literary and rhetorical devices to convey her arguments and insights. These include metaphor, allusion, irony, rhetorical questions, and vivid imagery. She also draws on a variety of sources, including historical documents, philosophical treatises, and literary texts, to provide a rich and nuanced account of the colonial experience.

Overall, "The Draught" is a powerful and insightful essay that challenges the dominant narratives of colonialism and provides a compelling vision of decolonization and resistance. Its themes and techniques continue to resonate in contemporary discussions of post-colonialism, critical race theory, and social justice.
