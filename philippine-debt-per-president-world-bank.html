3. Until when a country's debt to the world bank and International Monetary Fund has to be paid? ​


Answer:

The repayment period for a country's debt to the World Bank and International Monetary Fund (IMF) varies depending on the specific terms and conditions of the loans or assistance received.

Generally, loans from the World Bank and IMF have different repayment schedules ranging from 10 to 40 years, with a grace period of a few years before the repayments begin. The repayment terms and conditions are usually negotiated between the borrowing country and the lending institutions based on factors such as the economic condition of the country, the type of loan or assistance provided, and the repayment capacity of the country.

In some cases, the repayment period can be extended, or the debt can be restructured or forgiven altogether if the country is experiencing economic distress or is unable to pay back the loan. However, such arrangements usually require negotiations and agreement between the borrowing country and the lenders.

5. In the early 1970s, bank loans accounted for only 13 percent of third world debt, while multilateral loans made up more than 33 percent. At the end of the decade, the composition of these figures had reversed, with banks holding about 60 percent of the debt.PAKI EXPLAIN PO PLEASEE​


Answer:

In the early 1970s, only a small portion (13%) of debt owed by Third World countries was in the form of bank loans. The majority of the debt (33%) was in the form of loans from multilateral organizations, such as the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank. However, by the end of the decade, the situation had changed dramatically. Banks held a much larger share (60%) of the Third World debt, while the share of multilateral loans had decreased. This shift in the composition of Third World debt from multilateral loans to bank loans had a significant impact on the financial stability and development of these countries.

8. Why is the philippines debt rising?


Answer:

Explanation:

The Philippines' debt has been rising due to various factors, including increased government spending, borrowing for infrastructure projects, and external economic factors such as fluctuations in currency exchange rates and global economic conditions.

One of the main reasons for the rising debt is the government's ambitious infrastructure development program, which requires significant funding. To finance these projects, the government has increased borrowing from domestic and international sources. While this investment in infrastructure is essential for economic growth and development, it has also led to an increase in the country's debt.

Another factor contributing to the rising debt is external economic conditions. For instance, the COVID-19 pandemic has led to a global economic slowdown, causing the Philippine economy to contract, which has resulted in decreased revenue collection and increased borrowing to support government spending.

Finally, the depreciation of the Philippine peso against the US dollar has also contributed to the rising debt. This is because a significant portion of the country's debt is denominated in US dollars, and as the peso weakens, it takes more pesos to repay each dollar of debt, increasing the country's debt burden.

In summary, the Philippines' debt is rising due to a combination of factors such as increased government spending, borrowing for infrastructure projects, external economic conditions, and currency exchange rate fluctuations.
