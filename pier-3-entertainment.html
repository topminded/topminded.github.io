13. Ano ang kahalagahan ng pier


A pier is a raised structure in a body of water, typically supported by well-spaced piles or pillars. Bridges, buildings, and walkways may all be supported by piers. Their open structure allows tides and currents to flow relatively unhindered, whereas the more solid foundations of a quay or the closely spaced piles of a wharf can act as a breakwater, and are consequently more liable to silting. Piers can range in size and complexity from a simple lightweight wooden structure to major structures extended over 1600 metres. In American English, pier may be synonymous with dock.

17. A person stands at the end of a pier 10 ft above the water and pulls in a rope attached to a buoy. If the rope is hauled in at the rate of 3 ft/min, how fast is the buoy moving in the water when it is 7 ft from the pier?​


Answer:

0.7373 ft/min²

Step-by-step explanation:

(Kindly refer to the attached Image for the illustration)

Let us start with the given:

Distance 1 = 10ft

Distance 2 = 7ft

Velocity = 3ft/min

What is being asked is the acceleration of the buoy.

Acceleration is defined as the change in velocity over the change in time.

acceleration = [tex]\frac{Final velocity - Initial Velocity}{final time - initial time }[/tex]

For this problem, it is safe to assume that the initial velocity and initial time will be zero since the buoy started at rest initially before being pulled.

thus

acceleration = [tex]\frac{velocity}{time}[/tex]

* To solve the acceleration, we need the following:

Velocity - giventime - unknown

Now, let's get down to business.

STEP 1 : Solve for the TIME

velocity = [tex]\frac{distance}{time}[/tex]    (observe the formula, we can actually solve time)

*The distance that we need to solve is the distance that the buoy will be travelling, which is actually the length of the rope, I have labeled it as Z in my illustration. (Kindly refer to the 2nd image for the illustration)

The given distances actually forms a triangle.

Using the Pythagorean Theorem which states that the square of the length of the hypotenuse is equal to the sum of the squares of the two sides.

c² = a² + b²

where c is the hypotenuse and a and b are the two sides.

Going back to our problem,

Z is actually the hypotenuse and 7ft and 10ft are the two sides

Substituting:

c² = a² + b²Z² = 7² + 10²Z² = 49 + 100= 149[tex]\sqrt{Z^{2} }[/tex] = [tex]\sqrt{149}[/tex]  = Z = 12.20655 ft

* This is the distance we need

NEXT

velocity = [tex]\frac{distance}{time}[/tex]    manipulate the formulatime x velocity = distancetime = [tex]\frac{distance}{velocity}[/tex]    (since the only unknown is time)time = [tex]\frac{12.20655}{3}[/tex]  = 4.0689 min

ALMOST THERE!

acceleration = [tex]\frac{velocity}{time}[/tex] acceleration = [tex]\frac{3}{4.0689}[/tex] acceleration = 0.7373 ft/min²

Conclusion: The Buoy is moving at 0.7373 ft/min²

22. The water level fell 12.5 ft from a high tide to a low tide. The water level at the pier was 9.25 ft at low tide. Find the water level at the pier at high tide. PLEASE HELP ME, ASAP!


Answer:

[tex] \sqrt{4552} [/tex]

Answer:

21.75 ft

Step-by-step explanation:

To find the water level at the pier at high tide, we can use the information given that the water level fell 12.5 ft from high tide to low tide, and the water level at the pier was 9.25 ft at low tide.

We know that:

High tide - Low tide = 12.5 ft

So, if we add the low tide level to the difference between high tide and low tide, we can find the high tide level:

Low tide + (High tide - Low tide) = High tide

9.25 ft + 12.5 ft = 21.75 ft

Therefore, the water level at the pier at high tide is 21.75 ft
