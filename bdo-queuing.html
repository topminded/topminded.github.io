Answer:

Queuing theory is the mathematical study of the congestion and delays of waiting in line. Queuing theory (or "queueing theory") examines every component of waiting in line to be served, including the arrival process, service process, number of servers, number of system places, and the number of customers—which might be people, data packets, cars, etc.

12. Consider the queuing delay in a router buffer, where the packet experiences a delay as it waits to be transmitted onto the link. the length of the queuing delay of a specific packet will depend on the number of earlier-arriving packets that are queued and waiting for transmission onto the link. if the queue is empty and no other packet is currently being transmitted, then our packet’s queuing delay will be zero. on the other hand, if the traffic is heavy and many other packets are also waiting to be transmitted, the queuing delay will be long.


Answer:

yes, that is correct. The length of the queuing delay of a specific packet in a router buffer will depend on the number of earlier-arriving packets that are queued and waiting for transmission onto the link. If the queue is empty and no other packet is currently being transmitted, then the packet's queuing delay will be zero. On the other hand, if the traffic is heavy and many other packets are also waiting to be transmitted, the queuing delay will be longer.

This delay is also known as the "Queueing delay" which is the amount of time that a packet spends waiting in the buffer before it can be transmitted. This delay is caused by network congestion, where the router's buffer is full and packets have to wait for other packets to be transmitted before they can be sent. The queueing delay is an important metric in network performance and can be used to determine the quality of service (QoS) provided by the network.

Queuing System Using Android Technology definition of terms​

A collection for storing components before they are processed. Queues allow additional insertion, extraction, and inspection procedures in addition to fundamental Collection activities. There are two versions of each of these methods: one throws an exception if the operation fails, and the other returns a special value (either null or false, depending on the operation). The latter form of the insert operation is designed for usage with capacity-restricted Queue implementations; insert operations cannot fail in most implementations.

Queues usually sort elements in a FIFO (first-in-first-out) sequence, however this is not always the case. Priority queues, which order elements according to a specified comparator or the elements' natural ordering, and LIFO queues (or stacks), which order elements LIFO, are examples of exceptions (last-in-first-out).

26. Are there situations or circumstances where queuing and waiting lines are not applicable?​


Answer:

Are there situations or circumstances where queuing and waiting lines are not applicable?

Waiting in line at a bank or a store.

Answer:

Yes, there are situations or circumstances where queuing and waiting lines are not applicable. Here are some examples:

1. Online shopping: With the advent of e-commerce, customers can purchase products online without having to wait in a physical queue.

2. Reservation systems: Many businesses, such as restaurants and hotels, use reservation systems to avoid queues and waiting lines.

3. Appointment-based services: Services that require appointments, such as doctor's visits, haircuts, and spa treatments, eliminate the need for customers to wait in a queue.

4. Self-service kiosks: Self-service kiosks allow customers to complete transactions without having to wait in a queue.

5. Fast-track services: Some businesses offer fast-track services that allow customers to skip the queue and receive priority service. Examples include airport lounges and express checkouts at supermarkets.
