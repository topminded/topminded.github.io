16. what caused bagyong Ondoy to happen?​


[tex] \huge \color{lavender}{ \boxed{Answer:}}[/tex]

Bagyong Ondoy was a complex weather phenomenon that resulted from a combination of atmospheric and oceanic factors, as well as the topography of the affected region.

[tex]\huge \color{lavender}{ \boxed{Explanation:}}[/tex]

Bagyong Ondoy, also known as Typhoon Ketsana, was caused by a combination of factors, including warm sea surface temperatures, high humidity levels, and the presence of favorable atmospheric conditions in the Western Pacific. These conditions allowed for the formation and intensification of the typhoon.

In particular, during its development and approach towards the Philippines in September 2009, Bagyong Ondoy encountered an area of weak wind shear, which is the difference in wind direction or speed at different altitudes. This allowed the typhoon to maintain its strength and intensity for a longer period, leading to heavy rainfall and flooding in many parts of Luzon, the largest island in the Philippines.

The heavy rainfall produced by Bagyong Ondoy was exacerbated by the topography of the affected areas, specifically the mountainous terrain of the Sierra Madre range, which runs along the eastern side of Luzon. The mountains caused the typhoon's rain bands to converge over certain areas, leading to intense and sustained rainfall that resulted in widespread flooding and landslides.

29. when did ondoy leave the par


Answer:

Typhoon Ketsana, known in the Philippines as Tropical Storm Ondoy, was the second-most devastating tropical cyclone of the 2009 Pacific typhoon season, causing $1.15 billion in damages and 921 fatalities, only behind Morakot earlier in the season, which caused 789 deaths and damages worth $6.2 billion. Ketsana was the sixteenth tropical storm, and the eighth typhoon of the season. It was the most devastating tropical cyclone to hit Manila,[1] surpassing Typhoon Patsy (Yoling) in 1970.

Ketsana formed early about 860 km (530 mi) to the northwest of Palau on September 23, 2009. The depression remained weak and was downgraded to a low pressure area later that day by the Japan Meteorological Agency (JMA) and after drifting through extremely favorable conditions, it intensified the next day and was categorized as Tropical Depression by the Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration (PAGASA) and was given the name Ondoy after entering the Philippine Area of Responsibility. The Joint Typhoon Warning Center (JTWC) issued a Tropical Cyclone Formation Alert on the depression. It was then upgraded to a tropical depression by the JMA later that morning before the JTWC followed suit early on September 25, designating the depression as 17W. Soon, Ketsana was upgraded to a tropical storm before passing over the Philippines. As it moved into the South China Sea the storm intensified while moving toward the west, and was categorized as a Severe Tropical Storm by the JMA.

President Gloria Macapagal Arroyo declared a "state of calamity" encompassing most of Luzon after at least 86 people were initially reported dead in landslides and other incidents.[2] Flood water levels reached a record 20 feet (6.1 m) in rural areas. As of October 24, 2013, at least 464 deaths in the Philippines were officially reported from the typhoon.[3][4][5][6][7][8][9]
