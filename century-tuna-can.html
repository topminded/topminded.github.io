2. contituent elements of century tuna​


Answer:

The elemental composition of canned tuna fish packed in oil and brine was determined using the particle induced X-ray emission (PIXE) technique. Three different brands representative of the Brazilian market were evaluated. The metallic cans were analyzed as well. The results indicate that the canned tuna is homogeneous (average variability ∼5%) inside the cans as far as elemental concentration is concerned. The data analysis reveals that the major elements present in tuna fish are Na, Cl, K, P and S with average concentration ranging from 800 to 2400 mg/kg, while Fe (14.22 mg/kg) and Zn (4.51 mg/kg) constitute trace elements. Significant variations in the elemental concentration of canned tuna across the brands were observed for most of the elements. For instance, significant higher concentrations of Mg, P, K and Zn were observed for brand P when compared to the other brands. Moreover, significant differences were observed between oil-packed and brine-packed tuna. In this case, higher elemental concentrations were obtained for oil-packed tuna. An increasing pattern in the concentration of Fe as a function of storage time was observed for two of the brands studied in this work. Finally, the results concerning the metallic cans identified two different types of cans, namely Fe-rich cans and Al-rich cans. In general, the results obtained in this work are in good agreement with previous measurements of canned tuna.

14. Tina buys 64 canned goods, 48 of which are tuna flakes. What percent of the canned goods are tuna flakes?


Answer:

To find the percentage of canned goods that are tuna flakes, you need to divide the number of tuna flakes by the total number of canned goods and then multiply the result by 100%. This can be written as:

Step-by-step explanation:

(number of tuna flakes / total number of canned goods) * 100%

Plugging in the given values, you get:

(48 / 64) * 100% = 0.75 * 100% = 75%

So 75% of the canned goods are tuna flakes.

Answer:

Odor-active compounds from two canned tuna (albacore and skipjack) were fractionated and studied by gas chromatography/olfactometry-mass spectrometry (GC/O-MS). Aroma compounds were isolated using solvent extraction followed by solvent-assisted flavor evaporation and fractionation. A total of 51 odorants were identified. The sulfur-containing compounds (2-methylthiophene, 2-methyl-3-furanthiol, 1-acetyl-1-pyrroline, ethyl thioacetate, dimethyl sulfide, dimethyl trisulfide, 2-furfurythiol, methional, methionol) to meaty, chicken-like aroma, which are positive attributes to canned tuna aroma. The majority of the odor-active compounds, however, were saturated and unsaturated aldehydes such as hexanal, nonanal, (Z)-4-heptenal, (E,Z)-2,6-nonadienal, (E,Z)-2,4-decadienal, (E,E)-2,4-decadienal, ketone (1-octen-3-one) and acids (acetic, propanoic, butanoic, isovaleric, hexanoic and octanoic acids). Most of these compounds were identified in both skipjack and albacore species, but their levels were different between the two types of samples from five different manufacturing origins or harvest time.
