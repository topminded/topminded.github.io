Cultural relativism is a tool that was developed within anthropology for the purposes of better understanding cultures in as objective and unbiased way as possible. Cultural relativism doesn’t tell people how to behave in their everyday lives, it tells anthropologists how to think and behave as social scientists in order to better eliminate cultural bias and assumptions from their observations and analysis.

This is sometimes misunderstood and there is something of a minor ‘moral panic’ around the idea. Some people think that cultural relativism makes morality meaningless either because:

it prevents groups or individuals from passing moral judgement on each other (eg. forcing them to tolerate harmful behaviour when it occurs in a different culture)

or it is the belief that morality is whatever people think it is, according to personal preference or conscience, leading effectively to moral nihilism

There actually is a philosophy which does correspond to 1. which is called ‘normative ethical relativism’ and a problematic and possibly self-defeating idea (there are other varieties of moral relativism that have no such implications). And one that corresponds to 2. called ‘moral subjectivism’ which is equally nonsensical. However, these aren’t really very widespread ideas and pose no real threat to society."

27. explain the valuation monetary and Unpriced values​


Answer:

Valuation of monetary values refers to the process of assigning a specific monetary value or price to a product, service, asset, or investment. This is typically done based on factors such as market demand, supply, competition, and other market conditions. Monetary valuation is often used in financial analysis to determine the profitability, return on investment, and other financial metrics of a business or investment.

Unpriced values refer to goods or services that are not assigned a specific monetary value, such as natural resources, social and environmental benefits, and cultural heritage. These values are often difficult to quantify or monetize, but they play a significant role in the overall well-being and long-term sustainability of societies and ecosystems. Unpriced values are often not reflected in economic transactions, such as GDP, and therefore are not given sufficient consideration in policy and decision-making processes. However, there are ongoing efforts to develop methods for quantifying and valuing unpriced values, such as ecosystem services and social impact assessments.
