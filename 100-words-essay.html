While this data is all well and good, here to me is where some of our thinking about the Universe breaks somewhat with logic. The common scientifically published 'age of the universe' is 13.798 ± 0.037 billion years old, which goes on to say that Universal Expansion is the reasoning behind this dating when correlated with the radius of the known Universe.

Personally, I think the Universe is *much* older than that, possibly countless trillions of years old, and my reasoning behind that is pretty simple really: We can detect a radius of approx. 46G-ly, but how long have we had instruments trained on the region of space that we determine to be the edge of this Universe? When we look really deep into the void, our instruments detect the light of objects already in existence, not objects forming, but already existing. If the Universe were only 13-to-15 billion years old as some have suggested, then logic would dictate that the farthest away objects observable due to the speed of photonic motion (light-speed) would have the appearance of an accretion process or something similarly along those lines which would be in line with the (so called) laws of physics at the cosmic scale. However, I've never read a single article out of the cosmology community that made any such claims. Of course, with something so distant, could we even hope to get the level of detail to discern what objects at that distance are doing?

What is the Universe? If considered along the lines of quantum mechanics, then evidence shows the fundamental makeup of the Universe to be arrangements of energies that transcend the observable reality in which we find ourselves. One of the best examples of this is the most simple molecule we know... hydrogen. Hydrogen has 1 proton and 1 electron. As the electron orbits the proton, it disappears rather than making a full orbit, but yet when the electron reappears, it's perfectly in line with the orbital path that it left before disappearing. The big question here is where does this electron go?

I tend to put that information in the same category as "Where did the void come from in which we find our Universe?". A great mystery with no foreseeable answer in sight, at least not at our current level of technological progression. Maybe we'll understand these things better by the time we become a Type-2 or Type-3 civilization, but so long as we are a Type-0 civilization, these answers will remain out of reach.

Biomolecule, also called biological molecule, any of numerous substances that are produced by cells and living organisms. Biomolecules have a wide range of sizes and structures and perform a vast array of functions. The four major types of biomolecules are carbohydrates, lipids, nucleic acids, and proteins.

Portion of polynucleotide chain of deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA). The inset shows the corresponding pentose sugar and pyrimidine base in ribonucleic acid (RNA).

Among biomolecules, nucleic acids, namely DNA and RNA, have the unique function of storing an organism’s genetic code—the sequence of nucleotides that determines the amino acid sequence of proteins, which are of critical importance to life on Earth. There are 20 different amino acids that can occur within a protein; the order in which they occur plays a fundamental role in determining protein structure and function. Proteins themselves are major structural elements of cells. They also serve as transporters, moving nutrients and other molecules in and out of cells, and as enzymes and catalysts for the vast majority of chemical reactions that take place in living organisms. Proteins also form antibodies and hormones, and they influence gene activity.

All biomolecules share in common a fundamental relationship between structure and function, which is influenced by factors such as the environment in which a given biomolecule occurs. Lipids, for example, are hydrophobic (“water-fearing”); in water, many spontaneously arrange themselves in such a way that the hydrophobic ends of the molecules are protected from the water, while the hydrophilic ends are exposed to the water.

1. The National Service Training Program (NSTP) Act of 2001 (R.A. 9163) was enacted in response to public clamor for reforms in the Reserved Officers Training Corps (ROTC) Program. This act affirms that the prime duty of the government shall be to serve and protect its citizens. In turn, it shall be the responsibility of all its citizens to defend the security of the State; thus, the government may require each citizen to render personal, military or civil service. The NSTP is a program aimed at enhancing civic consciousness and defense preparedness in the youth by developing the ethics of service and patriotism while undergoing training in any of its program components.
