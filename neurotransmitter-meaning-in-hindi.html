1. What is the difference between neurotransmitter reuptake and neurotransmitter diffusion?​


Neurotransmitter reuptake is when the whole neurotransmitter molecule is brought back to the axon of the neuron from where it was released while in neurotransmitter diffusion, the neurotransmitter glides away from its receptor.

Neurotransmitters are also called chemical messengers. they are essential for our bodies to function well. They carry messages in the form of chemical signals from one neuron to a target cell. Once a neurotransmitter is done with its message, it can be stopped by any of these three:

Degradation: The structure of the neurotransmitter will be changed by an enzyme so it won't be recognized by the receptor.Diffusion: The neurotransmitter glides away from the receptorReuptake: The whole neurotransmitter molecule is brought back to the axon of the neuron from where it was released

Neurotransmitters are a system that is complex and highly connected with each other. They play an important role in the communication of our mind to different cells and organs inside our body.

7. Transport mechanisms is used to transport this neurotransmitter


Systems for Vesicular and Plasma Membrane Transport

The simultaneous activity of plasma-membrane and vesicular-transport systems results in the removal of amine and amino acid messengers from the synaptic cleft and their retention in cytoplasmic vesicles.

Chemical messengers called neurotransmitters that help nerve cells (neurons) in the nervous system communicate with one another. The signal is transmitted across the synapse, which is the tiny space between two neurons, via the release of these molecules into the synapse and their binding to receptors on the receiving neuron.The movement of neurotransmitters throughout the body is governed by a number of systems. Active transport, which involves molecules crossing the cell membrane against their concentration gradient, is one of the key mechanisms. ATP provides the energy necessary for this action, which is carried out by transporter proteins. There are many kinds of transporters, such as uptake transporters and release transporters, which take neurotransmitters out of the synapse and deliver them back to the presynaptic neuron.

In general, the transport systems involved in the transit of neurotransmitters are crucial for preserving adequate neuronal communication in the central nervous and are crucial for the body's proper operation.

The Major Stress Hormones:

1. Adrenaline – this is popularly known as the hormone responsible for “fight or flight”. Remember “adrenaline rush” too? This is produced by our body’s adrenal glands after receiving the message from the center of nervous system, brain, that one nerve-wracking situation has presented itself.  Adrenaline along with norepinephrine is really responsible for immediate reactions that we feel when we are stressed.  Along with the increase in heart rate, this adrenaline in our body could also give us surge of energy which tells us that we might have to escape such dangerous situation and it can also focus our attention.

 

2. Norepinephrine – is another hormone but also similar to our adrenaline. This is released from our adrenal glands and then it is also from our brain. Again, neurotransmitter. Its main role is also like adrenaline. Experts say that when a human body and brain is stressed, it will become more aware, much more awake, and ultimately focused. Because of this, we are becoming more responsive. Norepinephrine helps to shift our blood flow away too especially from the areas where it might not be so crucial and then toward more essential areas at the time (example: skin to muscles), thus we can run away from a very stressful scene.

 

3. Cortisol – this one is a steroid hormone which is popularly known as our stress hormone in the body produced by our adrenal glands. This takes a little more time for human beings to feel the effects of this cortisol in the face of stresses.

Imaging this, in our survival mode, the optimal amounts of this cortisol can in fact be a life-saving hormone because it helps the body to maintain such fluid balance as well as blood pressure while it regulates some human body functions that are not that crucial in the very moment (such as the reproductive drive, our immunity, the digestion and our pituitary or growth).

 

However, too much cortisol can unfortunately suppress our immune system as well as increase our blood pressure and sugar level. It could also decrease the libido plus produce acne or pimples and then contribute to serious body issues like obesity.

19. HOW DOES NEUROTRANSMITTER AND DIET AFFECT THE BEHAVIOR OF A PERSON?​


Answer:

process that particularly affects the release of amine neurotransmitters depends on changes in the composition of the blood plasma induced by eating or by prolonged physical activity. Changes in plasma levels of choline or of certain amino acids lead to changes in brain levels of the precursors for these neurotransmitters—choline for acetylcholine, tryptophan for serotonin, and tyrosine for the catecholamines. These, in turn, regulate the rates at which the transmitters are synthesized, their concentrations within nerve terminals, and ultimately, the quantities released each time the neurons fire. For one transmitter—serotonin—the relevant variations in plasma composition probably affect most, if not all, of the neurons that release it. For other transmitters (e.g., the catecholamines), individual nerve cells can become more or less precursor dependent at any time, depending on the rates at which they happen to be firing.

Unlike the receptor-mediated presynaptic modulation of transmitter release, precursor-dependent modulation depends primarily on metabolic events occurring outside the brain and arising from a particular type of voluntary behavior, such as eating or exercise. Indeed, the primary physiological role of this dependency may be sensory (i.e., to provide the omnivore's brain with information about what has been eaten or about important changes in macronutrient requirements, so that the individual can better decide what to eat next). However, because precursor-dependent neurotransmitters are involved in a wide variety of normal (and pathological) brain mechanisms besides those controlling food intake, this relationship may have broad physiological and medical implications. It also provides benign ways of influencing neurotransmission, and thus mental and physical performance.

27. Effects of alcohol,nicotine,drugs to our neurotransmitter and behavior.​


Effects of alcohol,nicotine,drugs to our neurotransmitter and behaviorAlcohol, nicotine, and drugs can all affect our behavior by altering the levels of neurotransmitters in our brains.

Alcohol:

Alcohol is a depressant that can affect the levels of neurotransmitters such as GABA, serotonin, and dopamine. GABA is an inhibitory neurotransmitter that can slow down activity in the brain, leading to sedation and relaxation. Serotonin is involved in regulating mood, appetite, and sleep, and alcohol can increase its release, leading to temporary feelings of happiness and well-being. Dopamine is involved in the reward pathway of the brain, and alcohol can increase its release, leading to feelings of pleasure and euphoria. However, chronic alcohol use can lead to a decrease in dopamine levels, which can contribute to addiction and withdrawal symptoms.

Nicotine:

Nicotine is a stimulant that can affect the levels of neurotransmitters such as acetylcholine, dopamine, and serotonin. Acetylcholine is involved in regulating attention and memory, and nicotine can increase its release, leading to improved cognitive function. Dopamine is involved in the reward pathway of the brain, and nicotine can increase its release, leading to feelings of pleasure and euphoria. Serotonin is involved in regulating mood, appetite, and sleep, and nicotine can increase its release, leading to temporary feelings of happiness and well-being. However, chronic nicotine use can lead to a decrease in dopamine levels, which can contribute to addiction and withdrawal symptoms.

Drugs:

Different drugs affect different neurotransmitters in different ways, but many drugs can increase the release of dopamine in the brain's reward pathway. This can lead to feelings of pleasure and euphoria, which can contribute to addiction. Some drugs can also interfere with the reuptake of neurotransmitters, leading to prolonged activation of the brain's reward pathway. This can contribute to addiction and can also affect other brain functions, such as mood regulation, cognitive function, and motor control.

Overall, the effects of alcohol, nicotine, and drugs on neurotransmitters and behavior can vary depending on the substance, dose, and duration of use. While some substances can lead to temporary feelings of pleasure and euphoria, chronic use can lead to addiction and long-term changes in brain function. It's important to be mindful of the potential risks associated with substance use and to seek help if you're struggling with addiction.

what part of the neuron passes neurotransmitter?​

The transmitting part of the neuron is the axon, which is a long, thin structure in which action potentials are generated. Action potentials travel down axons after initiation to cause neurotransmitter release.

One long cable snakes away from the main part of the cell in each neuron in your brain. This cable, several times thinner than a human hair, is called an axon, and it is where electrical impulses from the neuron travel away to be received by other neurons.

Depending on the type of neuron, axons greatly vary in length - many are just a millimetre or so, but the longest ones, such as those that go from the brain down the spinal cord, can extend for more than a metre.
