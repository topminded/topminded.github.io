This variety of a length, width, and volume problem can be generalized with 5 "cubic" blocks. A cube has 8 congruent square faces so to find what we are really looking for, (which is the thickness of a layer of paint that preserves the solid object, but not the volume of the solid 5 blocks if adding the additional volumes of the coatings of paint) we must derive the value of the volume of paint that will evenly cover each block if they are separated or perhaps if they are touching in a given number of possible combinatorial arrangements. As order here would not matter to indicate a permutation of touching block arrangements, we can analyze 5 blocks separated and painted over and are not found touching for our purposes.

The quick answer is all 5 blocks can be painted over with the pint of paint, we just want to know how thick the coating is. 1 pint of paint is equivalent to .4732 Liters of fluid volume, which in turn is equivalent to a volume contained by .001 m3 of contained space, in a solid similar to our 5 cubes. As cubic meters are inherently describing cubing regions in physical space, we are now conveniently able to divide the following cubic centimeter volume equivalent of paint by 5, to know how much paint to use on each block.

.001m3 = 1dl (one decaliter), and 1 dl = 103 cm3 = 1,000 cm3 with 1,000 dl = 1 m3

meaning (1,000)(1,000) cm3 = 1 m3 substituting 1,000 cm3 for 1 dl in the last equation

(1 pt)(.4732 L/1 pt.)(1 m3/1,000 L)( 1,000,000 cm3) = 473.2 cm3

This is how many square centimeters of paint we have available. Divided into 5 equal volume coats will mean (473.2/5) cm3 = 94.7 cm3 volume of paint per block.

If each 8 sided cubic block is 164.62 cm2 in total area, then (164.62/8) cm2 = 20.5775 cm2 is the area of each of its individual square faces. This allows us a way to find the edge length by taking the square root of its area, and unpainted volume by cubing the resulting value we get for the edge, or (20.5775 cm2)1/2 ≈ 4.5362 cm per edge,

then cubed becomes 93.345 cm3

Indeed we are adding slightly more than the original volume of each block to each block in terms of their volumes, in paint. So each of our 5 cubes will more than double, slightly, in volume after being painted, (with lost volume of the paint after drying being treated as negligible here.)

To calculate how much we approximately increase edge lengths of each of these cubes by spatially dilating them in a sense, simply take the cube root of the painted square volume, being the sum of paint and block volume scalar quantities per painted block combination, then subtract the original unpainted block edge length.

{(94.7 + 93.4) cm3}1/3 = 5.73 cm

Every edge of our 5 cubic blocks is increased from 4.54 cm to 5.73 cm.

(5.73/4.54) = 1.26

Painting 5 blocks with a pint of paint that can cover 5 square meters to some depth, we can instead paint 5 cubic blocks evenly to increase every edge by 26% roughly, thereby slightly more than doubling their volumes in doing so.
