2. A gas tank holds 2785L of propane ,C3H8, at 830. mm Hg. What is the volume of propane at standard pressure?


Good Day

Problem:

A gas tank holds 2785L of propane ,C3H8, at 830. mm Hg. What is the volume of propane at standard pressure?

Note:

At STP (standard temperature and pressure), pressure is 1 atm which is equal to 760 mmHg.

Given Data:

V₁ (initial volume) = 2785 L             V₂ (final volume) = ? unknown

P₁(initial pressure) = 830 mmHg     P₂ (final pressure) = 1 atm or 760 mmHg

Solution:

This problem can be solve using Boyle's Law in which volume is inversely proportional to pressure when temperature and the amount of gas are constant. When pressure decreases, volume increases.

The formula of Boyle's Law is   P1V1 = P2V2

To find V₂, we can use V₂ = P₁V₁ / P₂

P₂ = P₁V₁ / V₂

    = 830 mmHg  × 2785 L / 760 mmHg   (cancel unit mmHg)

    = 2,311,550 L / 760

    = 3041.51 L

Answer:

Final volume is 3041.51 L


3. a gas tank holds 2785 L of propane, C3H4, at 830 mm Hg. What is the volume of the propane at standar pressure?


Answer:

The volume of propane at standard pressure is 3041.513 L.

Explanation:

This problem is an application of Boyle's Law. Boyle's law is a gas law that describes the relationship of volume and pressure of a gas. It states that the pressure of a gas is inversely proportional to its volume at constant temperature. The mathematical expression for this law is as follows:

P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

where:

P = pressure and V = volumeP₁ and V₁ = initial conditions P₂ and V₂ =  final conditions

This equation will be used to get the final volume that the problem is asking for.

The problem gave us the initial volume, initial pressure, and final pressure.

Given:

P₁ = 830 mmHgV₁ = 2785 LP₂ = standard pressure

Standard pressure is equal to 1 atm or 760 mmHg.

Solution:

P₁V₁ = P₂V₂V₂ = [tex]\frac{P1V1}{P2}[/tex]V₂ = [tex]\frac{(830 mmHg) (2785 L)}{760 mmHg}[/tex]V₂ = 3041.513 L

The final volume of the gas in the tank at standard pressure is 3041.513 L

Note that we used 760 mmHg for the standard pressure for unit consistency.

10. A sample of propane (C3H8) gas was burned with excess oxygen gas. If 4.20 L of CO2 were produced from the reaction of at STP, how many liters of propane were used in the reaction?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Write the balanced chemical equation.

C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O

Step 2: Calculate the volume of propane used in the reaction.

Based on the balanced chemical equation (assuming that the conditions are the same for C₃H₈ and CO₂), 1 L of C₃H₈ is stoichiometrically to 3 L of CO₂.

[tex]\begin{aligned} V_{\text{C}_3\text{H}_8} & = \text{4.20 L} \: \text{CO}_2 \times \frac{\text{1 L} \: \text{C}_3\text{H}_8}{\text{3 L} \: \text{CO}_2} \\ & = \boxed{\text{1.40 L}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 1.40 L of propane were used in the reaction.

17. A gas tank holds 278 L of propane (C3H8) at 831 mmHg. What is the volume of the propane at standard pressure? Using GRESA method please


The GRESA (Given, Required, Equation, Solution, and Analysis) method is:

Given:

Gas volume = 278 L

Gas pressure = 831 mmHg

Required:

Gas volume at standard pressure

Equation:

P1V1 = P2V2 (where P1 and V1 are initial pressure and volume, and P2 and V2 are final pressure and volume)

Solution:

1. Convert the pressure to standard pressure units (1 atm = 760 mmHg):

831 mmHg / 760 mmHg/atm = 1.093 atm

2. Plug in the values into the equation:

(1.093 atm)(278 L) = (1 atm)(V2)

3. Solve for V2:

V2 = (1.093 atm x 278 L) / 1 atm = 303.79 L

Analysis:

The volume of the propane at standard pressure is 303.79 L.

21. Propane gas (C3h8) and oxygen (O2) at the same temperature are contained in pressurized tanks. Calculate the relative rate of diffusion and determine which gas diffuses faster.​


Given:

[tex]\text{gases} = \text{C}_{3}\text{H}_{8} \: \text{and} \: \text{O}_{2}[/tex]

Unknown:

relative rate of diffusion

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the molecular weight of C₃H₈ and O₂.

For C₃H₈

[tex]MW_{\text{C}_{3}\text{H}_{8}}[/tex] = (12.01 g/mol × 3) + (1.008 g/mol × 8)

[tex]MW_{\text{C}_{3}\text{H}_{8}}[/tex] = 44.094 g/mol

For O₂

[tex]MW_{\text{O}_{2}}[/tex] = 16.00 g/mol × 2

[tex]MW_{\text{O}_{2}}[/tex] = 32.00 g/mol

Step 2: Calculate the rate of diffusion.

[tex]\frac{rate_{\text{C}_{3}\text{H}_{8}}}{rate_{\text{O}_{2}}} = \sqrt{\frac{MW_{\text{O}_{2}}}{MW_{\text{C}_{3}\text{H}_{8}}}}[/tex]

[tex]\frac{rate_{\text{C}_{3}\text{H}_{8}}}{rate_{\text{O}_{2}}} = \sqrt{\frac{\text{32.00 g/mol}}{\text{44.094 g/mol}}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\frac{rate_{\text{C}_{3}\text{H}_{8}}}{rate_{\text{O}_{2}}} = 0.8519}[/tex]

Step 3: Determine which gas diffuses faster.

Based on the relative rate of diffusion obtained,

[tex]\boxed{\text{O}_{2} \: \text{diffuses faster than} \: \text{C}_{3}\text{H}_{8}.}[/tex]
