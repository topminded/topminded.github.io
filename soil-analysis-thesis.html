14. Difference between thesis and thesis statement


Answer:

Your thesis is the main idea, point, incident, or argument of your paper. That is in your mind.

Your thesis statement is that idea expressed in a sentence. An important caveat: this sentence must conform to the class or the reviewer’s (professor’s) requirement. If there is a specific form required, that should have been explained to you and taught to you before you have to write it. In one of my classes, stdents were required to create thesis statements, bring them to class, and have the critiqued in class discussions. We also studied examples of both good and bad thesis statements from published papers.

Thesis — idea.

Thesis statement — a sentence giving the thesis (idea) which conforms to the established form stating that idea.

Critical Literary Analysis: Atonement by Ian McEwan

Introduction:

Atonement is a novel written by Ian McEwan and published in 2001. McEwan is a British novelist and screenwriter. The novel takes place in three different time periods: 1935 England, during World War II, and 1999. The main theme of Atonement is the consequences of one's actions and the power of guilt and regret. The story follows the lives of the Tallis family and their interactions with the family's housekeeper's son, Robbie Turner. The thesis statement is that Atonement is a powerful exploration of the cost of deception and the difficulty of achieving redemption.

Body:

The novel's main character, Briony Tallis, is the catalyst for much of the tragedy that occurs in the story. Briony is a precocious thirteen-year-old with a vivid imagination and a desire to become a writer. Her misinterpretation of events leads her to accuse Robbie Turner of a crime he did not commit. This sets off a chain of events that ultimately leads to Robbie's imprisonment and separation from Cecilia Tallis, the woman he loves. The guilt of her actions haunts Briony for the rest of her life, and she spends years trying to make amends.

The novel's structure is particularly interesting. The first part of the novel takes place in 1935, and it is written in a third-person omniscient point of view. However, the second part of the novel takes place during World War II and is written in a first-person point of view, from the perspective of Robbie. This change in perspective is crucial to the novel's themes of guilt and redemption. Through Robbie's eyes, we see the cost of Briony's actions and the difficulty he faces in trying to atone for a crime he did not commit.

The third part of the novel takes place in 1999 and is once again written in a third-person omniscient point of view. This section is particularly poignant, as we see an elderly Briony reflecting on her life and her attempts to atone for her past misdeeds. The novel's final revelation is both surprising and heartbreaking, and it highlights the power of guilt and the human desire for redemption.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, Atonement is a powerful exploration of the cost of deception and the difficulty of achieving redemption. Through the character of Briony Tallis, Ian McEwan shows us the devastating consequences of one's actions and the lasting impact of guilt and regret. The novel's structure is particularly effective, as it allows us to see the story from multiple perspectives and to understand the characters' motivations and struggles. Overall, Atonement is a beautifully written and emotionally powerful novel that is well worth reading.
