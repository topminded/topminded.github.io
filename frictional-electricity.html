2. what is the meaning of friction electricity​


Answer:

Friction electricity, also known as triboelectricity, is a type of electricity that is generated by rubbing two materials together.

Explanation:

When two different materials are rubbed together, electrons can transfer from one material to the other, creating a buildup of electrical charge. This can result in static electricity, which can cause sparks or shocks.

Friction electricity is commonly observed in everyday life, such as when walking on a carpet and then touching a metal object, or when rubbing a balloon on hair to make it stick to a wall. It is also used in some industrial processes, such as electrostatic precipitation in air pollution control.

Answer:

Friction electricity, also known as static electricity, is the build-up of electric charges on the surface of a material due to friction or rubbing. When two materials are rubbed together, electrons can be transferred from one material to another, creating a static charge on the surface of each material. This static charge can then attract or repel other materials, causing them to stick together or separate. Friction electricity is responsible for many common phenomena, such as the attraction of a balloon to hair after being rubbed on clothing, or the shock you may feel after walking across a carpet and touching a metal object.


3. what electricity can caused friction


If two materials with a difference in their electron retaining powers (like glass/plastic/rubber and wool) are rubbed together, the friction does "work" and transfers electrons from one to the other and creates a difference in electric potential energy (p.d. or simply voltage). When the two come into contact again (possibly through a resistor), electrons are transferred back to the material from which they originally came. This "going back" is an electric current and can usually be seen in the form of a spark if they are brought into direct contact. 

Otherwise, friction can produce heat which can theoretically be used to generate electricity using a thermopile, but you need a very cold heat sink for that and practically you will end up getting 0.01 volts of p.d. from a million thermocouples and like negligible power due to what we call the "second law of thermodynamics", and you will use up more electricity to cool the cold end than a million such generators will produce.

13. Define the following Electric charge InductionFriction​


Answer:

Electric charge:

Electric charge refers to a fundamental property of matter that describes the presence of electrically charged particles, such as protons and electrons. Charged particles exert an electromagnetic force on each other, and the magnitude and direction of this force depends on the amount and sign of the electric charge. Electric charge is measured in Coulombs (C) and is a scalar quantity, meaning that it has only magnitude and no direction.

Induction:

Induction refers to the process by which an electric field can produce a current in a conductor without direct contact between the two. This is achieved by bringing a charged object near a conductor, which causes a redistribution of charges within the conductor, resulting in a net movement of charges and the creation of an induced current. Induction is an important concept in electromagnetism and is used in many electrical devices, such as transformers, generators, and motors.

Friction:

Friction is a force that opposes motion between two surfaces that are in contact with each other. It arises due to the irregularities in the surfaces of the objects that are in contact, and the frictional force depends on the nature of the surfaces and the normal force pressing them together. Friction can be both advantageous and disadvantageous in different situations. For example, it is necessary for walking or driving a car, but can also cause wear and tear on machinery or result in energy losses in mechanical systems.
