1. The art of communication is the language of leadership"​


Answer:

Communication is the Language of Leadership

Explanation:

It was American author and presidential speechwriter, James Humes who first coined the phrase, “the art of communication is the language of leadership” – and it’s my contention that while effective communication of leadership to a team or organisation is a learned skill, leadership characteristics are inherent traits and include a superior grasp of communication to be continually developed.

If language is our ability to learn complex systems of communication, leadership is the action of leading a group or organisation, and the two are inextricably linked. The leader who is so in title only will quickly run into trouble, if s(he) doesn’t learn how to communicate his/her ideas and expectations to their team(s), and then to expect them to deliver precisely that.

Absent communication is the absence of leadership but it’s not merely the presence or the quantity of information dispersed throughout your organisation that signifies whether or not you’re communicating effectively, it is as much about the quality and delivery of timely and relevant information. From what I have seen, leaders rarely use non-verbal cues to imply, nudge, hint, wink, or urge teams to act, their communication style is direct and they will articulate the purpose and direction of any action.

First-tier leaders understand the difference between catering (pandering) to the lowest common denominator and leading their flock in unison. The consummate leader has presence and communicates his or her leadership without resorting to introducing Machiavellian conditions in the workplace; this particular breed of leader possesses values, unique characteristics and skills and s(he) is focussed on developing future visions, a focus they spend a lot of time communicating to their teams, which inspires, motivates and channels support. If you run a tight ship with Swiss watch precision, the accomplished leader still manages to lead with integrity and maturity and has no need for self-aggrandising attempts at glory, on the contrary, a true leader will encourage, share and give credit where it’s due. It’s more about giving others the opportunity to succeed, never forgetting that the responsibility for getting things done lies with you. You won’t hear a leader bloviating about his/her accomplishments, s(he) will support, praise and discipline as necessary, much like a parent would a child, wanting to improve the child’s station in life by instilling values, a stellar work ethic and means of self-improvement, predicated on the ironclad conviction that you can absolutely achieve greatness if you work hard. Elements that will, in turn, be the pathway to a sustainable future for your business. And since we all live imperfect lives and have (more than) a few chinks in the armour, most leaders shun perfectionism because it’s an illusion and striving for it will inevitably disappoint.

Much like the conductor, the leader realises that the language of communication isn't a magic wand you can wave at every problem but an instrument to be used to marshal the energy and motivation of your team. Do it right, and the delegation or orchestration – to extend the metaphor – even of tedious tasks will be a cinch; you won’t have to dragoon your team into taking on undesired duties, they will appreciate why certain jobs are necessary to reach set objectives, which is a good thing as hovering like a jaybird is time consuming and rarely conducive to achievement.

As leader you are in a unique position to influence not only results but also the level of your team’s engagement and commitment. The bona fide leader would be disingenuous at best naïve at worst to not recognise that absent communication is a pressure cooker for the team as well as for anyone who has taken on the awesome responsibility of leading others. But in the mercurial world we inhabit, I think that when you are spearheading an organisation, you should be prepared for the inevitable scenario that most of the time, about half of the people will disagree with you, half of those will chastise you for it and precious few will commend you for your efforts.

I think ambition is good. I think overreaching is good. I think leaders should be optimistic, because, really, what’s the alternative? If you are a leader, you are a teacher not a tyrant and you have mastered the art of communication and appreciate that to lead is to take on a stewardship in service of the people you lead as much as the company you run.

5. leadership/handling their community-prayer life​


Answer:Early in my vocational ministry, our team was launching a new program that needed many volunteer leaders. We tried various recruiting techniques, begged and pleaded, and did everything we could to get people on board. When our efforts did not yield quick results, I was stressed over what to do next and afraid of failing. Then during a time of prayer, God brought Matthew 9:36-38 to mind. This was exactly the type of situation Jesus was talking to his disciples about. When the need is great and resources are limited, Jesus instructs us to ask the “Lord of the harvest” to provide.

In this “aha” moment, God reminded me that this was His church, His people, His mission, and ultimately, God would provide according to His plan. Moreover, the Holy Spirit eased my fear by reassuring me of my identity as a child of God, and my calling as a ministry leader. Whew! I felt such relief and a sense of peace. It was not up to me to persuade people into serving or growing, or even wanting to grow, that was up to God. My part was to pray and obey; doing my best to carry out whatever next step God has called me to do. Seems so simple and yet, this is a lesson I have had to relearn over and over again.

It is indisputable that prayer is essential to life as a follower of Jesus Christ. This is even more critical when we are entrusted to lead others. As leaders, it is our role to take people from where they are, to a better place, where they have not yet been and are not even sure they want to go. Without God’s constant guidance in our lives, how can we be confident of what we are teaching, how we are ministering, or why we are even leading? It does not matter how smart, well-intentioned or competent we are because our knowledge, skills, and talents are simply not enough for the task of leading others spiritually. That role is just too big for us to handle without continual prayer.
