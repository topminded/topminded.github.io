Parthenogenesis is also known as incomplete sexual reproduction.

Parthenogenesis (from Greek parthenos, "virgin", + genesis, "making") is the growth and development of an embryo or seed without fertilization by a male. Parthenogenesis occurs naturally in several species, including lower plants, invertebrates (e.g. water fleas, aphids, and some bees), and vertebrates (e.g. some reptiles, fish, and, very rarely, birds, and sharks).

About Parthenogenesis

Parthenogenesis is a form of asexual reproduction in which the female produces eggs that develop without going through the fertilization process. Parthenogenesis can be seen in aphids, bees, water fleas, and some other invertebrates, as well as in some plants. Komodo dragons and sharks are also capable of parthenogenesis, along with several genera of fish, amphibians, and reptiles - which have shown different forms of asexual reproduction, including true parthenogenesis, gynogenesis, and hybridogenesis (imperfect forms of parthenogenesis).

The alternation between parthenogenesis and sexual reproduction is called heterogamy. Forms of reproduction that are related to parthenogenesis but require sperm are called gynogenesis and hybridogenesis.

-Gynogenesis

The form of asexual reproduction that communicates with parthenogenesis is gynogenesis. Offspring are produced by the same mechanism as in parthenogenesis, but with certainty that the egg must be stimulated by the presence of sperm so that it can develop. However, sperm cells do not contribute any genetic material to offspring.

In hybridogenesis reproduction, reproduction is not strictly asexual but hemiclonal
