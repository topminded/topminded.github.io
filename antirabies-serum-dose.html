7. Average human blood serum contains 18mg of K+ and 365mg of CI- per 100mL. Calculate.a. The molar concentrations for each of these species; use 1.00g/mL for the density of serum. b. pK and pCI for human serum​


Answer:

a. To find the molar concentrations of K+ and Cl- in human serum, we need to first calculate the number of moles of each ion per 100 mL of serum.

For K+:

mass of K+ = 18 mg = 0.018 g

molar mass of K+ = 39.10 g/mol

moles of K+ = mass/molar mass = 0.018 g / 39.10 g/mol = 4.60 x 10^-4 mol

For Cl-:

mass of Cl- = 365 mg = 0.365 g

molar mass of Cl- = 35.45 g/mol

moles of Cl- = mass/molar mass = 0.365 g / 35.45 g/mol = 1.03 x 10^-2 mol

Next, we convert these moles to molar concentrations by dividing by the volume of serum (100 mL) and multiplying by 1000 to convert from L to mL:

Molar concentration of K+: (4.60 x 10^-4 mol / 0.1 L) x 1000 mL/L = 4.60 mM

Molar concentration of Cl-: (1.03 x 10^-2 mol / 0.1 L) x 1000 mL/L = 103 mM

b. To find the pK and pCI for human serum, we need to take the negative logarithm of the molar concentrations of K+ and Cl-, respectively:

pK = -log(4.60 mM) = 2.34

pCI = -log(103 mM) = 1.01

SERUM AND VACCINE

The vaccine is a preventive measure and the serum is a corrective measure, as they differ in the response time in the body.

Difference between serum and vaccine:

The purpose of the vaccine is to induce long-term protection through memory cells so that our own body is able to produce antibodies. On the other hand, serum is used in cases of rapid solution, that is, ready-made antibodies are provided without their production by the body.

The immune system guarantees protection to the human body, but the vaccine and serum come in as an aid to stimulate an immune response against infections and diseases. Vaccines are dead antigens - processed or inactive - that support active immunization.

In this type of immunization, the patient will come into contact with an antigen or infectious agent, stimulating the immune system to produce antibodies artificially. Thus, after the vaccination process, the specific antibodies themselves will stimulate the memory cells against that disease or infection.

In addition, another form of immunization is the so-called passive. In this case, there will be no memory stimulation by the antibodies, as ready-made antibodies will be administered with the aim of accelerating the immunity process. An example of passive immunization is the use of sera in accidents with snakes and other venomous animals that need a quick response for the production of antibodies.
